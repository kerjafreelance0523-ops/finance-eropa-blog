## Bitcoin quantum threat: mapping exposure and building a framework

The **Bitcoin quantum threat** is concentrated in outputs where public keys are exposed: legacy Pay-to-Public-Key outputs and reused addresses. CoinShares and CoinMarketCap suggest no systemic threat before the 2030s but increasing relevance thereafter. The first step for institutions is to build a quantum-aware risk framework: map all holdings across custodians and wallets, identify legacy P2PK outputs and reused addresses, and adopt baseline hardware timelines based on research from CoinShares and others. The Bitcoin P2PK vulnerability and the 4 million BTC exposed estimates (CoinShares quantum research) define the target set; the Satoshi 1M BTC narrative is a reminder of the stakes.

Address hygiene is the cheapest and most effective control. Mandates should require custodians to avoid reuse, report on UTXO composition, and have clear procedures for migrating funds away from legacy scripts as better options become available. ## Migration planning and communication

Waiting until million-qubit machines exist is not an option. Institutions should track NIST post-quantum standardisation, engage custodians on roadmaps for quantum-resistant outputs, and run small pilots with test funds on sidechains or testnets. By the time Bitcoin mainnet supports post-quantum signatures, leading allocators should have rehearsed the operational choreography.

Communication matters: disclose quantum risk to boards and clients in calm, quantified language—what portion of holdings are in exposed outputs, what the migration plan looks like, how often quantum research is reviewed. Referencing Binance, CoinShares, and CoinMarketCap anchors the discussion in data. Post-quantum cryptography and quantum-resistant wallets are the technical destination; the playbook turns the Bitcoin quantum threat from a source of anxiety into a dimension of professional risk management.
