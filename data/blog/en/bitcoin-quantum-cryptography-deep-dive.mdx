## Bitcoin quantum threat: signatures vs hashes

Shor's algorithm is the main concern for Bitcoin's signatures. It turns the discrete logarithm problem into something a large enough quantum computer could, in theory, solve efficiently. That means ECDSA and Schnorr signatures on secp256k1 are vulnerable if an attacker ever gains access to thousands of logical qubits with low enough error rates to run Shor's algorithm at scale. The signature layer is the weakest link in a quantum context. SHA-256, by contrast, is affected by Grover's algorithm, which can effectively cut a 256-bit hash down to about 128 bits of security—still astronomically strong.

For addresses that use Pay-to-Public-Key-Hash (P2PKH), the public key stays hidden behind a SHA-256 hash until coins are spent, forcing attackers to either break the hash or run Shor's algorithm fast enough in the brief mempool window. In practical terms, quantum computers are far more dangerous to long-lived, exposed public keys than to modern hashed addresses. Old P2PK outputs and heavily reused addresses are the softest targets; that is the **Bitcoin P2PK vulnerability**. The Satoshi 1M BTC wallets and the 4 million BTC exposed (CoinShares quantum research) are in this category. ## Why the narrative overstates a "hash break"

While many summaries suggest that quantum computers could "break Bitcoin's cryptography" in one sweep, a closer look at the algorithms shows that hashes are not the primary target. Post-quantum cryptography will focus on the signature side; SHA-256 can likely remain unchanged. New post-quantum signature schemes can be integrated via soft forks, as Taproot did with Schnorr. Once you see that the Bitcoin quantum threat is mainly a signature problem, the path forward becomes clearer: retire vulnerable signature schemes gradually, keep SHA-256, and treat quantum as a design constraint rather than an unstoppable disaster.

That distinction has practical implications for investors and builders. Migration efforts should prioritise moving value away from P2PK and address reuse; hashing infrastructure does not need a wholesale replacement. The EU monetary policy outlook and ECB interest rates 2026 demand the same kind of precision: separate what is at risk from what is not, and act on the former without overreacting to the latter.
