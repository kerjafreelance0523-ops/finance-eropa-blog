## Bitcoin quantum threat: where attacks would focus

CoinShares emphasises that the immediate exposure is limited to outputs where public keys are visible: mostly old Pay-to-Public-Key (P2PK) outputs and reused addresses. Binance adds that by 2026 that surface is more theoretical than exploitable. The **Bitcoin P2PK vulnerability** and the 4 million BTC exposed estimates (CoinShares quantum research) define the target set; the Satoshi 1M BTC narrative is the most dramatic example.

It is useful to sketch how a capable attacker might behave if a Bitcoin-breaking quantum machine did exist. The first scenario is a slow drip: quietly draining small amounts from long-dormant P2PK addresses over months or years. From the market's perspective, that would show up as a trickle of additional sell pressure—unpleasant but comparable to large holders gradually taking profit. A more dramatic scenario would target famous, long-silent addresses believed to belong to Satoshi or other early whales. The moment those coins move, on-chain analysts and social media would explode and a sharp, temporary sell-off would be likely. Yet such an event would not automatically destroy Bitcoin; it would more likely trigger a violent but absorbable repricing.

The technically scariest scenario is mempool sniping: deriving a private key while a transaction is waiting to be mined, then front-running the spender. That would require running Shor's algorithm in minutes, which is far beyond any hardware on the horizon. Long before that is realistic, Bitcoin is likely to have adopted post-quantum signatures. ## While doomsday headlines dominate, data points to limited impact

While many commentators suggest that a quantum computer could one day "empty" Bitcoin in a single stroke, the data from CoinShares and Binance tells a different story. The attack surface is concentrated in legacy outputs and reused addresses; the vast majority of actively managed coins sit in Pay-to-Public-Key-Hash or SegWit-style outputs where the public key appears only at spend time. That leaves a narrow mempool window for a hypothetical attacker—and current hardware is nowhere near the millions of physical qubits required to exploit it within one block interval.

The popular narrative often ignores the distinction between "theoretically at risk" and "practically targetable." CoinShares quantum research and estimates of 4 million BTC exposed include address reuse and one-time-revealed keys; only a fraction of that is in large, high-value UTXOs that would move markets if stolen. For investors, the implication is clear: the Bitcoin quantum threat is real over the long term, but panic is not a substitute for preparation. Migration to post-quantum-ready outputs and better address hygiene reduce exposure without requiring perfect timing.
