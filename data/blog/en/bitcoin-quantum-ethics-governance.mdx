## Bitcoin quantum threat and the burn-vulnerable-coins temptation

Whenever a new threat appears, proposals return: change the rules, override old outputs, and fix the problem by decree. Some have suggested burning or invalidating coins in legacy formats, assuming owners are gone. CoinShares warns strongly against this. Aggressive interventions that tamper with existing outputs without explicit owner consent risk undermining property rights and immutability. Today the target might be dormant P2PK coins; tomorrow it could be politically unpopular addresses.

We do not know which dormant coins are lost and which belong to long-term holders. The Satoshi 1M BTC narrative does not give anyone the moral right to confiscate assets based on guesses. The Bitcoin P2PK vulnerability is a technical reality; the response must respect consent and neutrality. ## Ethical approach: consent, communication, and choice

An ethical approach to the Bitcoin quantum threat respects consent, communication, and choice. Users should get clear information about risks and migration options years in advance but remain free to decide when and how to move coins into post-quantum outputs. Developers and institutions can nudge behaviour through incentives without unilaterally voiding valid UTXOs. Post-quantum cryptography and quantum-resistant wallets are the right technical direction; burning coins is not. A community that refuses to burn coins for convenience sends a powerful signal that neutrality, property rights, and immutability are not negotiable. That stance may slow some technical upgrades but preserves the trust that gives Bitcoin its value. For investors, the implication is that governance matters as much as technology; the same discipline used to assess ECB interest rates 2026 and eurozone inflation 1.9% should extend to how the ecosystem handles the Bitcoin quantum threat.
