The US 50/30/20 rule does not fit France. So why do Paris planners often beat London spenders when the CAC40 rallies? A 40/30/30 rule does.

Healthcare, mutuelle, and university costs take a larger share of the typical French budget than in the US, so “needs” run higher. Boursorama and Les Echos readers know the result: a rigid 50/30/20 leaves many French households either undersaving or feeling guilty. A 40/30/30 rule—40% living (including mutuelle and essential fees), 30% saving and investing, 30% lifestyle and playing—better matches French reality. When the ECB eases and the CAC40 and STOXX 600 rally, Paris planners who follow 40/30/30 tend to have a stable savings rate and can add to equities without cutting essentials. London or US-style 50/30/20, in contrast, often forces a choice between “needs” and “savings” that does not reflect French cost structure. This article explains why the 40/30/30 rule fits France and how it supports CAC40-focused portfolios in 2026.

## Why 50/30/20 fails in France: healthcare and fees

In the US, out-of-pocket healthcare and education costs are often 8–12% of income for middle-class households. In France, mandatory charges (social contributions, mutuelle, and direct education costs) can reach 15% or more before “discretionary” spending. So a 50% “needs” bucket in the US can cover housing, utilities, health, and basic transport. In France, the same 50% is stretched: housing, mutuelle, transport, and fees already push the needs share higher. Forcing 50/30/20 onto a French budget either reclassifies mutuelle as “wants”—which feels wrong—or squeezes savings below 20%. The outcome is that many French savers either abandon the rule or save inconsistently. The 40/30/30 frame fixes that by explicitly allocating 40% to living costs that include French-specific essentials. The remaining 60% is split 30% savings and 30% lifestyle, so savings get a dedicated 30% instead of fighting with needs for the last 20%.

The Conversation’s “Realist” and “Contemporary” EU money types fit this approach: pragmatic, adapted to local reality rather than importing a US template. Compass Europe’s high “Conscientiousness” (C) supports rule-based budgeting; the key is that the rule must match the country. So for Boursorama and Les Echos readers, the first step is to stop measuring yourself against 50/30/20 and to adopt a 40/30/30 (or similar) split that reflects your actual living costs. Once the rule is realistic, adherence improves and the 30% savings bucket can flow steadily into CAC40 or STOXX 600 exposure. ## 40/30/30 in practice: €3K living, €2K saving, €1.5K playing

A concrete example makes the rule tangible. Take a Paris household with €6,500 net monthly income. Under 40/30/30: 40% = €2,600 for living (rent or mortgage, utilities, mutuelle, transport, insurance, groceries). Thirty per cent = €1,950 for saving and investing (emergency fund, PEA, assurance-vie, pension top-up). Thirty per cent = €1,950 for lifestyle (dining, culture, travel, hobbies). In round numbers, that is €3K living (if we allow a slight rounding), €2K saving, €1.5K playing—with the rest absorbed by the 30% buckets. The point is that the 30% savings slice is protected. When the ECB cuts rates and the CAC40 rallies, this household can maintain or increase equity allocation without raiding the living bucket. A 50/30/20 budget with the same income would cap savings at 20% (€1,300), leaving €650 less per month for the market. Over a decade, that gap compounds into a large difference in wealth.

The French-style rule allocates more to savings and aligns “needs” with actual French cost structure. The result is a higher and more stable savings rate, which supports CAC40 and STOXX 600 investing when the ECB eases. For readers building a long-term plan, the message is to tailor the percentages to your country and then automate the savings share so that volatility does not tempt you to cut it.

## When 40/30/30 is not enough

While many personal-finance guides suggest that adopting 40/30/30 alone will put French households ahead, the data from savings rates and equity participation suggests that automation and discipline matter as much as the rule. A 30% savings bucket that is repeatedly raided for “one-off” expenses does not compound. So the rule is necessary but not sufficient: locking in the 30% via automatic transfers to a PEA or assurance-vie is what makes the difference. In addition, at very high or very low income levels, the fixed percentages may need adjustment; 40/30/30 is a template, not a universal law.

For Boursorama and Les Echos readers, the takeaway is to adopt 40/30/30 and then protect the savings bucket with automation and a clear separation between “playing” and “saving.” Without that, the rule can become another good intention that fails in practice.

## Timeline: Age 25 to 45 with 40/30/30

Life stage matters. At 25, “living” may dominate and the 30% savings bucket might go first to an emergency fund and then to a PEA or index fund. By 35, the same 30% is feeding a larger portfolio and perhaps a pension top-up. By 45, the trifecta—living, saving, playing—is in balance, and the investor has had 20 years of consistent allocation to equities. The 40/30/30 rule does not change with age; the content of each bucket does. Early on, “playing” might include more discretionary spending; later, “saving” might tilt toward retirement. The stability of the rule is what helps Paris planners beat ad-hoc London spenders: when ECB easing and CAC40 strength arrive, the French saver has been in the market all along, while the 50/30/20 follower in the UK may have under-saved or over-spent on “needs” because the rule did not fit. ## Conclusion and takeaways

The US 50/30/20 rule underfits France because healthcare, mutuelle, and fees push “needs” above 50%. A 40/30/30 rule—40% living, 30% saving, 30% playing—aligns with French cost structure and protects a 30% savings rate. In practice, that can mean €3K living, €2K saving, €1.5K playing on a €6,500 net income; the exact numbers depend on household size and city. The outcome is more stable savings and better participation in CAC40 and STOXX 600 rallies when the ECB eases. Paris planners who adopt 40/30/30 tend to beat London-style 50/30/20 followers because the rule is realistic and sustainable.

Two scenarios frame the outlook. In an optimistic case, French households that adopt 40/30/30 and automate the savings bucket build a CAC40-ready portfolio over 10–20 years and benefit fully when the ECB eases and equities rally. In a more conservative case, income volatility or life events force repeated raids on the 30% bucket; the rule still helps structure decisions, but the outcome depends on discipline. Takeaways: (1) Drop 50/30/20 if you are in France and adopt a 40/30/30 (or custom) split that includes mutuelle and fees in “living.” (2) Lock in the 30% savings bucket—automate transfers to PEA or assurance-vie so that lifestyle does not crowd it out. (3) Use the age timeline to adapt the content of each bucket while keeping the percentages fixed, so that by 45 you have a full trifecta and a CAC40-ready portfolio.
